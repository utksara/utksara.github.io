A brief introduction to Data-Driven Simulations

Almost anyone working right now in the tech or engineering domain must have heard the term "digital twin" by now at least once. A generic definition of a digital twin is a comprehensive, real-time visual representation of a real-world phenomenon, physical object, or system. There are several ways to interpret this definition, but it doesn’t provide a clear picture of what a digital twin is. Is it just another way of saying simulation? In that case, what is new here, since we have been using simulation software for industrial use cases for a long time? One can say the crucial aspect of a digital twin is real-time processing and simulation of data, which can create a hyperrealistic digital version of your setup. For example, if you have a coffee machine and you decide to build a digital twin of it, it will collect data in real time from the brewing process, including the precise amount of coffee bean, the amount of moisture content in them, the statistics of the size of coffee granules after grading, the humidity ofthe environment, hardness of water used in the brewing process, temperature and pressure, precise dimension of brewing instruments, and many more to list. The coffee prepared by the digital twin is expected to be almost identical to the coffee you prepare in real life. Obviously, there is no way to taste and check the digital coffee, but you can definitely measure the concentration of the aromatic compounds that give a coffee its flavor. Assuming the overall one-time cost to run the coffee machine digital twin is reasonably less than the price of coffee beans and the cost of operating the machine several times to figure out the right proportions of your coffee, a digital twin can give you exact values of all relevant parameters that make your coffee fit for your taste.

That was just a simple example of what a digital twin could look like. In conventional computer simulations, we typically use a small subset of a physical object's parameters to predict certain properties. Prediction is performed using known laws and equations governing the system's physics. As an example, to examine bending stress in a mechanical device, we just need to know its structural properties like Young's modulus, Poisson's ratios, and geometry. But in digital twins and data-driven systems, the other, in addition to combining voluminous amounts of a system's parameters to simulate multiple properties, also performs data-based prediction from an external feed. Purely law- and equation-based simulations are prone to errors and deviations from the real system, due to numerical errors and intricacies that mathematical equations cannot always capture. With the advent of data science, machine learning, and artificial intelligence over the last decade, it is possible to combine physics-based constraints with data to predict system properties with very high accuracy, which often improves with more data or context. However data data-driven approaches are also costly to implement, and there is always a chance we end up over-engineering our problem. A balanced intermediate could be a data-driven framework that provides the users complexity of a digital twin while maintaining the lightweight nature of traditional simulation frameworks.

CytoViz: A platform for data-driven and query-based simulation for Microbiology and material science

Try CytoViz yourself

Oftentimes, our requirements in simulations are not the generation of large amounts of data by predicting every possible aspect of the problem, but rather the storage and efficient analysis of our experimental data. For example, consider a research lab that conducts cell experiments every day and stores the data in Excel sheets. Over the course of a year, if laboratory staff members want to analyze data or recall the details of specific experiments, they can use an Excel sheet and other data analysis tools. But this is often a time-consuming process that involves extensive preprocessing and intermediate steps. Usually, the description of the problems is intuitively well developed in mind, but transforming them into Excel commands or MATLAB scripts can be tedious. Additionally, performing experiments is are vivid experience for humans. Plotting graphs and publishing figures do not provide the same level of insight as a visual setup of the experiment as it was performed. Humans can easily define their problems in words and queries. For example, “search all the experiments where cell shape was changing with time”, “search the experiment where the cell shrank when exposed to a certain drug.” A framework that can automate intermediate tasks by directly translating while also providing a query-based simulation framework with minimalistic visualization of the actual experimental setup would be a cost-effective and valuable solution to this problem.

In summary, we are developing a query-based simulation framework that combines data-driven approaches and physics to build a lightweight digital twin of laboratory experiments in microbiology and materials science. Our current product focuses on computational analysis of cell mechanics, which is the study of the mechanical properties and behavior of living cells and their correlations with cell function. Cell mechanics have crucial applications in cancer research and other non-pathogenic diseases. A typical experimental setup in cell mechanics involves observing a single cell or a small cluster of cells on a soft material substrate under a microscope. The position and shape of the cell are observed over time, along with the substrate on which the cell is prepared. Post-experimental analysis involves calculating the traction force (the force exerted by the cell in isolation on its environment) and changes in movement and shape over time. Current frameworks provide analysis tools for individual tasks, such as a traction force calculator and cell segmentation. However, there is no centralized tool for all cell mechanics calculations that provides these functionalities as a single module with a user-friendly interface. Additionally, most modules offer a GUI-based simulation or a pure coding-based simulation interface. While GUIs can be easy to learn, they offer little versatility, and some users may find GUIs cumbersome. On the other hand, a coding-based framework technically offers full flexibility, but deep-diving into a 3rd-party codebase and making modifications tailored to your needs can be extremely time-consuming and draining. Our Summelit CytoViz app offers a query-based simulation comprehensively covering cell mechanics tasks, allowing users to process, validate, and even generate virtual versions of their experiments. With the help of the Summelit CytoViz module, users can replicate their experimental setup almost immediately by uploading all relevant files in raw format (requiring no intermediate processing) and providing context for the data in human-readable language or via a query. In addition, users can create new experimental setups in CytoViz without physically setting up any equipment, using the same declarative paradigm that supports human-readable text and a bit of context in the form of files or text, thereby providing a convenient, intuitive, and faster experience for experimental design and analysis.
