Falling lights

He was working late at night in his small hometown in India. Tired enough that his thoughts no longer moved in straight lines, he sat in front of his laptop while the room around him stayed silent. Everyone in the house was asleep. Only the screen was awake, glowing softly as leaves fell in a looping animation. In that half-sleep state, the falling leaves pulled him somewhere else. They reminded him of old times—not even old, really. Only twelve or fifteen months ago. It was night in Gdańsk. The buildings and streets were yellow under the lamps, and the winter sky carried a dim orange hue from the clouds reflecting city light. There was no snow, but the cold still had teeth. It was somewhere between ten and midnight when he was walking home with his lover. Both of them had come from the same country, but they were living different chapters of the same story. His lover was more settled, calmer, already standing on firmer ground. He was not. He was still restless, still carrying hopes that had not yet found shape, still believing life was waiting just one step ahead. The streets were mostly empty. That emptiness made the city beautiful and frightening at once. Their footsteps echoed more than they should have. But they kept walking together toward home, toward the twelfth-storey apartment building where they lived on the eighth floor. They reached home. His lover changed, said little, and soon went to sleep. He said he would work for a while. He went into the kitchen of the one-bedroom apartment and turned on the yellow night light. It was just enough to give the room a dim glow against the darkness. He sat at the dining table, opened his laptop, and pulled the curtain away from the window. It was around eleven. From the eighth floor, he could see the buildings below and the same orange winter sky hanging over the city. Everything felt still. Suspended. Like life had paused only for him. He looked outside and thought to himself that maybe life would be like this from now on. Maybe this was what the future looked like. A foreign city, someone sleeping in the next room, work on the table, warmth inside, cold outside, and the feeling that somehow he had made it. He began the work he was supposed to do. Instead, he fell asleep there at the table. Then he woke up. The memory vanished as quickly as it had arrived. It had been real, but it belonged to another season. Now he was back in the present, sitting in his own room in India. His parents were asleep in the house. His lover was in another city now. They were still together, but those pleasant European days had passed into something harder to hold. He stared again at the laptop. He understood then how dangerous comfort can be. The moments he once took for granted, the nights he assumed would continue forever, had already become memories before he noticed them leaving. But another thought followed. The real reward is not in trying to preserve one perfect chapter. It is in doing your best wherever you are placed. In working honestly in the room you are in now. In staying loyal to your hopes even when life looks smaller than it once did. Unexpected good times do not announce themselves when they arrive. They slip quietly into ordinary evenings and only later reveal their value. One day, even this room may become something he misses. One day, perhaps, life may send him back to Gdańsk. Or somewhere else entirely. So he straightened himself in the chair, looked once more at the falling leaves on the screen, and returned to work.
